About

Designing technology around real people, workflows, and needs.

Hi there, my name is Yuhsing Wu. I am currently a Master’s student in Health Technology with a concentration in Human-Computer Interaction at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign. I also hold a Bachelor’s degree in Kinesiology and Health Science from The University of Texas at Austin.

My interest in UX research and design grew from my experience volunteering in a hospital, where I had to learn complex internal systems for user profiles, patient-related information, and workflow tasks. I noticed how confusing these systems could feel, especially for someone new to the environment. This experience helped me realize how important usable design is in fast-paced healthcare settings, where clinicians and staff need systems that support their workflow rather than slow them down. It shaped my interest in designing tools that reduce confusion, improve efficiency, and better support real user needs.

My background combines human factors, ergonomics, and UX research, with a strong interest in human-centered design. I am especially interested in understanding how people interact with technology and how thoughtful design can improve usability, accessibility, and overall user experience.

I am currently seeking full-time opportunities in UX research, UX design, and human factors engineering, where I can contribute to the design, testing, and improvement of user-centered products, digital health tools, medical devices, and other technologies that support real-world user needs.

Smartphone-Based Gait & Motion Analysis App

Ongoing project in collaboration with the UbiHealth Lab at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, focused on improving the usability and real-world feasibility of a smartphone-based gait and motion analysis app. The project supports accessible gait capture outside traditional laboratory settings by using smartphone cameras and built-in inertial sensors.

My role focuses on the human factors and UX components of the system, including reviewing the current app workflow, identifying key user tasks, defining usability questions, and preparing participant-facing evaluation materials. The project is designed to examine whether users can complete gait capture tasks correctly, confidently, and with minimal errors.

Planned work includes formative user research, task analysis, usability evaluation, workflow observation, and synthesis of findings into evidence-based UI/UX recommendations. Expected deliverables include documented user needs, workflow pain points, usability findings, and recommendations related to onboarding, instruction clarity, feedback, error prevention, and data capture reliability.

MindfulMeals — mHealth App for Cooking Support, Safety, and Independence

Designed MindfulMeals, a human-centered mobile health app concept to support adults experiencing early cognitive changes in completing cooking tasks more safely and independently. The project focused on reducing cognitive load during everyday meal preparation while supporting organization, sequencing, safety, and social connection.

Applied a structured human factors and user-centered design process including literature review, subject matter expert feedback, user needs identification, personas, journey mapping, ideation, and low- to mid-fidelity prototyping. Core features included a guided cooking flow, grocery list support, chatbot assistance, safety check prompts, and a community meal-sharing feature.

Presented this work at the HFES Health Care Symposium as part of the Student mHealth App Competition Finals. The concept was developed to address pain points in instrumental activities of daily living by emphasizing accessible design, independence, and kitchen safety for adults with mild cognitive impairment or early cognitive changes.

Human-Centered Tool for Predicting Hospital Length of Stay

Designed a human-centered decision-support prototype for predicting hospital length-of-stay categories using an interpretable machine learning workflow. Rather than focusing only on predictive accuracy, the project prioritized usability, low cognitive load, clear explanations, and quick interpretation for users such as care coordinators, clinicians, and hospital staff.

Built a Gradio-based interactive interface using an open-source hospital length-of-stay dataset. The interface allows users to enter a small set of patient and admission-related features, including age, severity of illness, type of admission, visitors with patient, and bed grade. The system outputs a predicted stay category and confidence score, using categorical results to make predictions easier to interpret in real-world healthcare settings.

Used a Random Forest model to generate length-of-stay category predictions while supporting interpretability through feature importance. Visualizations such as length-of-stay distribution, visitor count patterns, and feature importance were added to help users understand the prediction, interpret data patterns, and build trust in the system.

Smart Sensor Insole for Sit-to-Stand Rehabilitation

Designed and prototyped a low-cost smart sensor insole to support quantitative sit-to-stand assessment and real-time feedback in physical therapy and rehabilitation settings.

Conducted background and market research on fall risk, hospital readmissions, and existing gait and pressure-platform technologies to identify gaps in affordability, accessibility, and real-time feedback.

Designed and built a hardware prototype using force-sensitive resistors, Arduino, and embedded LEDs to capture plantar force distribution during sit-to-stand tasks. Applied human factors principles to optimize sensor and hardware placement, minimize discomfort, preserve natural gait, and improve user acceptance and compliance.

Evaluated prototype performance through iterative testing and refinement while balancing biomechanical validity, usability, and cost constraints.

Withings BPM Connect — Human Factors Evaluation

Conducted a comprehensive human factors evaluation of the Withings BPM Connect blood pressure monitor through comparative analysis, task analysis, and heuristic evaluation. The project examined usability, user workflow, potential use errors, cognitive load, and safety considerations for connected health monitoring devices.

Reviewed competing FDA-cleared devices, including Garmin, iHealth, and Omron, to compare usability, features, and reported issues. Created hierarchical task analyses and PCA tables for key user workflows such as pairing, measurement, and report generation.

Assessed the companion app using Nielsen’s usability heuristics and identified opportunities to improve feedback clarity, system status visibility, error prevention, and overall user experience.
