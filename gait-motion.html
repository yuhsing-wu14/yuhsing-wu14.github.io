Intro

A human-centered mHealth project focused on designing and evaluating a smartphone-based gait and motion analysis app for accessible mobility assessment outside traditional laboratory settings.

This ongoing project is conducted in collaboration with the UbiHealth Lab at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign. The app uses smartphone cameras and built-in inertial sensors to support gait and movement data collection in real-world environments. My work focuses on UX research, usability evaluation, workflow analysis, and preliminary TUG-based evaluation to improve instruction clarity, task completion, and data quality.

Skills

Human-Centered Design, Human Factors Engineering, mHealth Evaluation, UX Research, Usability Testing, Workflow Analysis, Task Observation, UI/UX Recommendations, Gait Analysis, Data Interpretation, Digital Health

My Role

My role focuses on the human factors and UX side of the smartphone-based gait assessment system. I am supporting the evaluation of how users interact with the app workflow, where they may experience confusion, and what design changes could improve task completion, instruction clarity, and data quality.

I am responsible for reviewing the app workflow, preparing usability questions and study procedures, observing participant interactions with the app, collecting feedback on instruction clarity and task completion, and translating findings into practical UI/UX recommendations.

I am also supporting a preliminary TUG-based evaluation by comparing manually collected reference measures, such as TUG time and potentially step count or cadence, with app-generated outputs from the same task. This helps connect the usability of the app workflow with the quality and usefulness of the gait data collected.

Problem

Gait analysis is valuable for assessing mobility, fall risk, rehabilitation progress, and functional performance. However, traditional gait analysis is often conducted in specialized laboratories using expensive equipment, trained personnel, and controlled testing environments.

These requirements can make gait assessment difficult to access, costly, and hard to repeat frequently. Because traditional assessments are usually completed in controlled lab environments, they may only capture a brief snapshot of a person’s movement rather than everyday mobility patterns.

Smartphone-based gait assessment has the potential to make mobility evaluation more scalable and accessible. However, real-world use depends on more than the technology itself. Users need to understand how to set up the phone, follow the task, complete recordings correctly, and know whether the captured data is usable.

Design question: How might we design and evaluate a smartphone-based gait assessment workflow that users can complete correctly, confidently, and with minimal errors?

Users / Stakeholders

The primary users are individuals completing gait or mobility tasks using the smartphone app, including adults with varying physical performance levels. These users may differ in mobility, comfort with technology, and familiarity with gait testing procedures.

Research and clinical stakeholders include researchers, clinicians, and health technology teams interested in accessible gait assessment, fall-risk evaluation, rehabilitation monitoring, and remote mobility tracking.

The project partner is the Ubiquitous Health Intelligence Lab at UIUC, directed by Dr. Yiwen Dong. The broader UbiGait project explores how everyday devices, such as smartphones, can support portable gait and motion data collection outside traditional laboratory settings.

Research or Design Methods

This project uses a combination of human factors, UX research, and health technology evaluation methods. The work began with a review of literature on gait analysis, mobile sensing, smartphone-based gait assessment, usability of mHealth tools, and real-world implementation barriers.

I reviewed the app workflow to identify key user tasks, potential confusion points, and possible sources of data collection error. This included thinking through the full gait capture process, from user preparation and phone setup to task completion and result review.

The usability evaluation is designed to assess whether users can understand the app instructions, complete the gait task correctly, and use the workflow with minimal support. Planned measures include task observation, usability questions, participant feedback, task completion, and user satisfaction.

The project also includes a preliminary evaluation using the Timed Up and Go test. Participants complete repeated TUG trials while manual reference measures, such as TUG time and potentially step count or cadence, are compared with app-generated outputs from the same task.

Early participant feedback may also support limited participant-informed refinement, such as improving instruction wording, screen order, button labels, task flow, metric explanations, or result visualization.

Process

The process began with understanding the clinical and practical value of gait analysis. Gait metrics can support mobility evaluation, fall-risk assessment, rehabilitation monitoring, and functional performance tracking, but traditional lab-based systems are often difficult to access or repeat frequently.

I then reviewed the existing smartphone-based gait capture workflow to understand how users move through the app, where they may need clearer guidance, and where workflow errors could affect the quality of collected data.

After reviewing the workflow, I prepared usability materials and study procedures focused on instruction clarity, task completion, user confidence, satisfaction, and independent use. The project is connected to an IRB-approved research process, so participant-facing activities are planned around approved study procedures.

The evaluation uses the Timed Up and Go test as a standardized functional mobility task. During each session, participants complete repeated TUG trials while manual/reference data and app outputs are collected during the same task.

Findings from usability sessions, participant feedback, workflow observation, and TUG-based comparison will be synthesized into actionable recommendations for improving onboarding, recording guidance, feedback, error prevention, result explanation, and overall data quality.

Key Findings / Outcome / Impact

Because this project is ongoing, final findings are still in progress. The expected outcome is a set of evidence-based usability findings and UI/UX recommendations that can support future refinement of the smartphone-based gait assessment workflow.

The project is expected to identify workflow pain points, unclear instructions, and barriers to correct task completion. These findings will help improve how the app guides users through phone setup, gait recording, repeated trials, and result review.

The preliminary TUG-based evaluation will provide early insight into how manually collected reference measures compare with app-generated outputs. This helps evaluate whether the app can provide useful gait-related information beyond timing alone.

By improving the usability and reliability of a smartphone-based gait capture system, this project may help reduce barriers to mobility assessment for people who cannot easily access specialized gait laboratories.

Reflection / Next Steps

This project is meaningful to me because it connects my interests in human factors, biomechanics, mobile health technology, and UX research. It allows me to apply human-centered methods to a real digital health system where usability directly affects data quality, accessibility, and potential real-world impact.

Next steps include completing usability sessions, analyzing participant feedback, reviewing TUG-based comparison results, and developing final recommendations for the app workflow.

Moving forward, I plan to focus on clearer user guidance during gait recording, more reliable task completion across users, better error prevention and recovery, more understandable result presentation, and evidence-based UI/UX refinement grounded in participant feedback.
