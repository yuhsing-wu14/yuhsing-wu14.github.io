Medical Device Evaluation

Withings BPM Connect Evaluation

September 2025 – December 2025

Usability Testing Task Analysis Heuristic Evaluation Comparative Analysis Medical Device Evaluation Human Factors

Description

Evaluated the usability, safety, and human factors considerations of the Withings BPM Connect, a connected blood pressure monitor designed for adult home use. This project examined the device and companion Health Mate app through comparative product review, task analysis, and heuristic evaluation to identify usability risks related to device setup, measurement accuracy, data interpretation, and report sharing.

Method / Process

I first conducted a comparative review of the Withings BPM Connect against other FDA-cleared upper-arm blood pressure monitors, including Garmin Index BPM, iHealth Track, and Omron Evolv. The comparison focused on device form factor, cuff design, power source, display characteristics, connectivity, portability, and usability.

I also reviewed FDA MAUDE reports to identify real-world issues such as inaccurate readings, connectivity failures, cuff discomfort, and device malfunctions.

I then completed a hierarchical task analysis for three core user workflows: pairing the device with the app, taking the first blood pressure measurement and syncing data, and generating/sharing a health report. For each workflow, I mapped perceptual, cognitive, and manual task demands and identified potential use errors, harms, and severity levels.

Finally, I conducted a heuristic evaluation of the Health Mate app using Nielsen’s 10 usability heuristics. The evaluation focused on common tasks such as device setup, pairing, measurement, result review, and report generation. Usability issues were categorized by heuristic violation and severity to prioritize design recommendations.

Outcome / Impact

The evaluation identified recurring usability risks around unclear system feedback, medical terminology, data interpretation, and error prevention. Key findings showed that users may struggle with pairing feedback, proper cuff placement, understanding color-coded results, interpreting blood pressure trends, and confirming report-sharing actions.

The heuristic evaluation identified 17 usability issues across four major tasks, with the most common violations related to system visibility and mismatch between interface language and users’ real-world understanding.

Based on these findings, I recommended clearer setup feedback, stronger posture and cuff-placement guidance, plain-language explanations for blood pressure values, improved report previews, and accessibility features such as larger text and simplified layouts for older adults.

Overall, the project strengthened my ability to apply human factors methods to home health technologies and evaluate how design decisions affect user confidence, safety, and accurate self-monitoring.

Rehabilitation Technology

Smart Sensor Insole

September 2025 – December 2025

Hardware Prototyping Arduino Force-Sensitive Resistors Human Factors Engineering Rehabilitation Biomechanics Iterative Testing

Description

Designed and prototyped a low-cost smart sensor insole to support sit-to-stand rehabilitation by tracking foot pressure and providing real-time visual feedback. The project addressed limitations in current rehabilitation assessment, where physical therapists often rely on subjective observation and may lack accessible tools for quantitative mobility tracking. The insole was developed as an affordable alternative to expensive pressure-platform systems and commercial smart insoles, with a focus on rehabilitation use, patient-clinician communication, and home-based monitoring.

Method / Process

Our team began by identifying the clinical problem: many balance and gait rehabilitation tools rely on qualitative therapist assessment, while precise center-of-pressure systems can be expensive and less accessible for routine or home-based rehabilitation. We reviewed existing technologies, including pressure-platform systems, commercial smart insoles, and wearable gait-analysis tools, to define the gap for a lower-cost rehabilitation-focused solution.

We then developed a conceptual prototype and moved into mid-fidelity hardware prototyping using an Arduino Mega, force-sensitive resistors, EVA foam, Velcro, jumper wires, and LED components. The prototype placed sensors under the heel and forefoot, areas that naturally bear load during standing and sit-to-stand movement. This placement was selected to support comfort, reduce pressure points, and minimize disruption to natural gait and stability.

For feedback, we implemented an LED feedback circuit linked to FSR readings. The LEDs were designed to light up when appropriate force was applied to the heel and forefoot, creating a simple form of real-time feedback for users and clinicians. We also researched sit-to-stand force thresholds, including heel-to-forefoot weight shift and left-right loading symmetry, to guide the logic behind calibration and feedback behavior.

The prototype went through iterative testing, including LED function testing, realistic force application, and comfort testing. During development, we adjusted the design based on technical constraints, including challenges with FSR calibration, threshold setting, Bluetooth implementation, and Raspberry Pi visualization. As a practical solution, we shifted the feedback approach toward LED-based visual feedback and adjusted the code to improve baseline readings.

Outcome / Impact

The final prototype demonstrated a feasible low-cost pressure-sensing insole that could detect force input and provide immediate LED feedback during sit-to-stand-related loading. The estimated prototype cost was approximately $60–$80, making it substantially more affordable than many commercial smart insole or pressure-platform systems.

From a human factors perspective, the project emphasized wearability, comfort, sensor placement, and rehabilitation usability. The prototype showed how quantitative pressure feedback could support more meaningful patient-clinician conversations, help track rehabilitation progress, and potentially support remote rehabilitation or continuous home monitoring.

The project also revealed important future design opportunities, including adding Bluetooth communication, building two synchronized insoles, incorporating Raspberry Pi visualization, adding buzzer feedback, conducting real user testing inside a shoe, and expanding to a larger sample size. Overall, this project strengthened my experience in connecting hardware prototyping, biomechanics, and human factors engineering to design accessible rehabilitation technology.

Biomechanics Analysis

How Shoe Inserts Affect Lower Extremity Squat Mechanics

February 2026 – May 2026

Biomechanics Motion Capture Force Plate Analysis OpenSim Inverse Kinematics Inverse Dynamics Lower-Extremity Mechanics

Description

Investigated how orthopedic shoe inserts may affect lower-extremity biomechanics during a bodyweight squat. The project focused on whether changes in underfoot support could influence ankle, knee, and hip joint angles and torques during a functional movement that requires coordinated lower-extremity control, balance, and body-weight support.

The study was motivated by the role of the foot and ankle as the primary mechanical interface between the body and the ground. Because inserts may alter plantar pressure, foot alignment, and force distribution, the project explored whether these changes could meaningfully affect squat mechanics and joint loading strategies.

Method / Process

Our team used a within-subject experimental design with four healthy adult participants. Each participant completed bodyweight squat trials under two footwear conditions: flat shoes only and flat shoes with orthopedic inserts. Each participant completed three trials per condition, for a total of six squat trials per participant.

Motion data were collected using a six-camera motion capture system, and participants performed a squat sequence that included standing, descent, a five-second hold, ascent, and return to standing. Lower-extremity motion was analyzed across the squat cycle, including the descent, hold, and ascent phases.

The analysis used OpenSim musculoskeletal modeling to evaluate hip, knee, and ankle mechanics. We examined joint angles through inverse kinematics and estimated joint torques through inverse dynamics. Ground reaction force data were incorporated to support the analysis of loading patterns during the squat task.

We also interpreted the results using biomechanical reasoning, including equations of motion and free-body diagram analysis for the foot, tibia, and thigh. This helped connect the computational model outputs to lower-extremity joint loading and muscle function during the squat.

Outcome / Impact

The analysis found no statistically significant differences in ankle, knee, or hip joint angles between the insert and no-insert conditions. Both footwear conditions showed similar squat movement patterns, including ankle dorsiflexion and knee and hip flexion during descent, followed by extension during ascent.

The inverse dynamics results also showed no statistically significant differences in joint torques between conditions. However, slight variations in knee and hip torque were observed during the hold phase, suggesting that inserts may still influence subtle loading strategies even when overall movement patterns remain similar.

Overall, this project strengthened my experience in motion capture analysis, OpenSim modeling, force plate interpretation, and lower-extremity biomechanics. It also highlighted the importance of sample size, marker quality, model assumptions, and future use of EMG or center-of-pressure analysis when evaluating how external support devices affect human movement.
